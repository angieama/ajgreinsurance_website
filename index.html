Keyman Insurance

Key person insurance is a life insurance policy that a company purchases on the life of an owner, a top executive, or another individual considered critical to the business.

Read More

The company is the beneficiary of the policy and pays the premiums. This type of life insurance is also known as "key man (or "keyman") insurance," "key woman insurance," and "business life insurance."

Key person insurance offers a financial cushion if the sudden loss of a certain individual would profoundly negatively affect the company's operations. The death benefit essentially buys the company time to find a new person or to implement other strategies to save (or shut down) the business.

In a small business, the key person is usually the owner, the founders, or perhaps a key employee or two. The main qualifying point is whether the person's absence would cause major financial harm to the company. If this is the case, key person insurance is worth considering.

Political Risks

Political risk insurance protects businesses, investors, and lenders against losses due to political risks in foreign countries such as expropriation, political violence, and currency inconvertibility.

Read More

Political risk insurance is a type of insurance designed to protect businesses, investors, and lenders against losses due to political risks in foreign countries. Political risks can include events such as government expropriation of assets, political violence, currency inconvertibility, contract repudiation, and other political events that can disrupt business operations or result in financial losses.

Coverage: Typically covers losses resulting from specified political events beyond the control of the insured party, including expropriation, political violence, currency inconvertibility, breach of contract by a government entity, and more.

Benefits: Provides financial protection in politically unstable or high-risk countries, helping mitigate risks associated with investing or doing business in these regions.

Types of Policies: Can be tailored for single projects or multiple investments, covering equity, loans, trade transactions, or whole portfolios.

Insurers: Offered by specialized insurers, government agencies, or multilateral institutions such as MIGA (World Bank) or OPIC.

Importance: Crucial for operations in regions with political instability, regulatory uncertainty, or weak rule of law.

Trade Credit & Investment Risks

Trade credit insurance protects businesses from the risk of non-payment by customers due to insolvency or protracted default.

Read More

Trade credit insurance, also known as credit insurance or export credit insurance, protects businesses from the risk of non-payment by their customers.

Coverage: Protects accounts receivable against non-payment due to buyer insolvency or protracted default. Typically covers a percentage of the insured amount.

Benefits: Helps manage non-payment risk, protects cash flow, and can enable better financing terms as insured receivables serve as collateral.

Cost: Depends on buyer creditworthiness, industry, country, and coverage limits.

Claims Process: File claim with evidence of default; insurer investigates and compensates if approved.

Valuable for domestic and international trade to ensure operational stability.

Energy and Construction Risks

Specialized coverage for energy sector exposures (machinery, business interruption, offshore damage) and construction project risks (liability, workers’ comp, builder’s risk, tools & equipment).

Read More

Energy Insurance: Protects energy companies against machinery repair, income loss due to business interruption, offshore physical damage, and related professional liability exposures.

Construction Risks: Protects construction companies, contractors, and property owners from lawsuits, property loss, and liabilities. Often requires multiple tailored policies.

Key Types of Construction Insurance:

  • General liability: Covers third-party property damage, bodily injury, advertising injuries, and completed operations.
  • Workers’ compensation: Covers medical expenses and lost wages for job-related injuries (mandatory with employees).
  • Commercial motor: Covers accidents involving company vehicles (trucks, etc.).
  • Builder’s risk: Covers damage to structures under construction (fire, weather, vandalism).
  • Contractor’s tools & equipment: Covers loss, theft, or damage to tools and equipment.
  • Professional liability (E&O): Protects against lawsuits over work quality or subcontractor performance.

Oil and Gas Risks

Specialized property, inland marine, and liability coverage for oil & gas operations including rigs, drilling equipment, pollution incidents, and third-party claims.

Read More

Businesses in the oil and gas industry typically need a combination of specialized property, inland marine, and commercial general liability coverages tailored to unique operational risks.

A property or inland marine policy can cover damage to buildings, rigs, drilling equipment, storage tanks, and more.

Commercial general liability provides coverage for bodily injury, property damage, and personal/advertising harm to third parties, including pollution-related incidents (sudden & accidental) and limited pollution cleanup costs.

Marine and Aviation Risks

Coverage for watercraft, aircraft, cargo, terminals, hangars, and related liabilities including marine cargo/transit and aerospace product liability.

Read More

Marine Insurance: Covers boats, yachts, ships, cargo, terminals, and onshore/offshore property. Excludes willful misconduct, ordinary leakage, wear and tear, inherent vice, and delay-related losses.

Aviation Insurance: Property and liability coverage for aircraft, hangars, and aerospace product manufacturers. Excludes intentional acts, criminal activities, wear and tear, or mechanical breakdown.

Marine Cargo and Transit Insurance: Protects goods in transit (sea or air) against theft, damage, and other threats under Goods in Transit (GIT) policies.

Delegated Authority Underwriting

Contractual arrangement where an insurer delegates underwriting, claims handling, or other functions to another party under a delegated authority relationship.

Read More

Delegated authority is an insurance industry term referring to the outsourcing of certain functions. It is a contractual arrangement under which one party authorizes another party to act on their behalf.

For example, an insurance company may delegate underwriting or claims handling to another party. These two parties then have a "delegated authority relationship."

Climate Risk Insurance

Financial risk transfer instruments protecting against losses from extreme weather events intensified by climate change.

Read More

Climate risk insurance describes a suite of instruments for financial risk transfer that protects against risks arising from extreme weather events that are increasing in frequency and intensity because of climate change.

It offers protection to regional entities, governments, institutions, companies, community groups, households, and individuals against loss of life, livelihood, or assets caused by extreme weather events. It ensures effective and rapid post-disaster payments.

While hazards are directly determined by climate change, vulnerability and exposure depend on socio-economic factors. Effective planning requires understanding these elements through climate risk assessment.

Parametric Agricultural Solutions

Index-based insurance that pays out based on predefined triggers (e.g., rainfall, temperature) rather than actual loss assessment.

Read More

Parametric insurance covers the probability of a predefined event happening (e.g. a major hurricane or earthquake), paying out according to a predefined scheme instead of a lengthy claim adjustment process.

Unlike traditional indemnity insurance, parametric insurance pays out when a predefined event occurs as measured by a specified parameter or index (flood, hurricane, earthquake, etc.).

It offers financial protection against losses that are often hard or impossible to insure traditionally, providing faster payouts without lengthy loss adjustment procedures.

CAT Modeling

Computerized simulation of thousands of plausible catastrophic events to quantify potential damages and insured losses using scientific, engineering, and statistical methods.

Read More

A catastrophe model simulates thousands of plausible catastrophic event scenarios based on realistic parameters from meteorological history, geology, and geography. These scenarios probabilistically model future events to quantify expected damages to a portfolio using engineering approaches, then calculate insured losses by applying policy terms.

Models provide insights for risk identification, quantification, and management through a multi-disciplinary approach (science, engineering, mathematics/statistics). Evolving since the 1980s with better technology and data, they now cover non-weather risks like liability, terrorism, and cyber-attacks.

Cat Model Basics: Quantify financial impact beyond historical data, incorporating latest scientific research. Estimate direct (physical damage, injuries), indirect (business interruption), and residual losses (demand surge, inflation).

Core Components (Risk Triangle):

  • Hazard: Event catalog with frequency, location, intensity, footprints, and annual probability.
  • Vulnerability: Damage functions relating hazard intensity to expected damage based on building characteristics.
  • Exposure: Portfolio data including location, risk details, insured values, and policy terms (deductibles, limits, reinsurance).
  • Financial: Calculates insured losses across scenarios, aggregates to create loss probability distribution, derives AAL and EP curves.

Outputs: Exceedance Probability (EP) curves (aggregate or occurrence), Average Annual Loss (AAL).

Return on Risk Adjusted Capital (RORAC)

A profitability metric that evaluates projects or investments by dividing net income by risk-weighted (or economic) capital, enabling comparison across different risk profiles.

Read More

The return on risk-adjusted capital (RORAC) measures rate of return while accounting for capital at risk. It is widely used to compare projects, divisions, or investments with varying risk levels.

Formula:

RORAC = Net Income / Risk-Weighted Assets

Risk-weighted assets = Allocated risk capital, economic capital, or value at risk.

RORAC adjusts for maximum potential loss based on earnings volatility or future distributions, providing a risk-sensitive view of performance.

IFRS Preparedness

Support for compliance with IFRS 17 Insurance Contracts, including aggregation, discount rates, risk adjustment, transition, and statutory reporting requirements in Kenya.

Read More

Section 54 of the Insurance Act mandates IFRS-compliant financial statements. IFRS 17 (effective 1 January 2023) replaces IFRS 4 and sets principles for recognition, measurement, presentation, and disclosure of insurance contracts.

Objective: Provide relevant, faithful representation of insurance contracts' impact on financial position, performance, and cash flows.

IRA Guidance (Kenya):

  • Level of aggregation: Portfolios by classes/subclasses per Insurance Act; discretion for similar risks managed together.
  • Discount rates: Bottom-up or top-down approach; justify choice; risk-free rate = government bond yield curve.
  • Risk adjustment: Disclose method and confidence level (minimum 75% if not using confidence interval).
  • Transition: Apply from 1 January 2023; submit restated 2022 IFRS 17 statements with Q2 2023 returns.
  • Statutory Reporting: Continue submitting statutory returns alongside IFRS 17.

Reinsurance Data Analytics

Improving data quality to reduce reinsurance costs and avoid penalties or coverage refusal in hardening markets — including risk profiles, large losses, treaty statistics, exposure aggregates, and projections.

Read More

High-quality data is critical for favorable reinsurance terms. Poor data can lead to higher pricing or coverage denial.

Key Data Requirements:

  • Risk and loss profiles: Group policies/claims by sum insured bands.
  • Large losses & CAT events: Historical large claims above threshold and CAT-affected policies (include below-deductible for inflation-adjusted analysis).
  • Historical reinsurance experience: Treaty statistics/results (premiums ceded, commissions, recoveries) — varies by proportional vs non-proportional.
  • Catastrophe exposure aggregates: Sum insured by peril, occupancy, coverage type, and location (postal code, CRESTA zones, lat/long for facultative).
  • Revised & projected premiums: EPI/GNPI revisions for past years and projections for new period (proxy for exposure growth).

Good data quality signals professionalism and responsiveness — leading to better reinsurance outcomes.

Additional Reinsurers Information & Challenges

Other requested items (rate changes, special risks, business plans, retentions) plus common data quality challenges and improvement actions for insurers.

Read More

Other Items Reinsurers May Request:

  • Primary rate changes (especially for CAT-exposed classes).
  • List of special risks requiring special acceptance.
  • High-level business plans.
  • Maximum retentions by class (non-proportional).
  • Current/expiring reinsurance structures.

Common Data Quality Challenges (especially emerging markets):

  • Legacy systems unable to meet evolving needs.
  • Lack of dedicated data resources.
  • Insufficient knowledge of reinsurers' data expectations.

Recommended Actions to Improve:

  • Train staff on reinsurance data needs (brokers can provide templates and awareness).
  • Allocate dedicated resources for data management.
  • Foster collaboration between IT and reinsurance teams.

Insurers with strong data practices are viewed favorably and often secure better terms and capacity.
